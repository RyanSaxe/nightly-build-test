Nvidia: The Toll Booth of the AI Era

Nvidia sells the shovels for the AI gold rush — the question for an owner is how long the miners stay locked to this one supplier.

12 min read2026-07-07

What you are actually buying

Nvidia is no longer a chip company in any meaningful sense; it is the landlord of AI compute. In its fiscal 2025 — the year ending January 2025 — it reported $130.5 billion of revenue, up 114% year on year, of which $115.2 billion came from a single segment: the data center.1 A year later, fiscal 2026 revenue reached $215.9 billion, with $193.7 billion of it from data center.6 No company of this size has ever grown this fast, and almost none has ever concentrated so much of its fortune in one product line sold to a handful of customers.

For a capital allocator, three questions decide whether this is a franchise or a bubble, and this dossier takes them in order. How good is the data-center business, really? What actually stops a competitor from taking it — the "moat," named precisely rather than admired vaguely? And what is the one risk the moat cannot cover: the US government's power to decide who Nvidia may sell to. A patient owner needs a defensible answer to all three.3

The data-center machine

Begin with the economics, because they are the whole story. In fiscal 2025 the data-center segment grew 142% to $115.2 billion; the next-largest segment, gaming, was $11.35 billion, with professional visualization at $1.88 billion and automotive at $1.69 billion.19 Data center is not the largest part of Nvidia; it is roughly nine-tenths of it, and everything else is a rounding error by comparison.

The growth has been close to vertical. Quarterly data-center revenue climbed from $18.4 billion at the start of calendar 2024 to $62.3 billion by the quarter ending January 2026 — more than a tripling in two years.67

That concentration cuts two ways an owner should weigh. The revenue leans on a small number of very large buyers — the hyperscale cloud providers — and Nvidia's own 10-K flags significant customer concentration and a heavy geographic tilt, with a large share of sales billed through a handful of countries.3 A business whose demand sits with perhaps five customers, several of whom are simultaneously building rival chips, is powerful and fragile in the same breath: any one of them trimming orders moves the whole company.

What makes the segment remarkable is not the growth but the margin at which it grows. Nvidia's GAAP gross margin rose to 75.0% in fiscal 2025, from 72.7% the year before, which the company attributed to a richer mix of data-center sales.3 A hardware maker earning 75 cents of gross profit on every revenue dollar, while doubling volume, is a company facing no effective price competition. The reason it faces none is the subject of the next section — but the caveat that comes with it is visible in the same filings. Management has been guiding margins back toward the low-70s as it ships more complex, lower-margin rack-scale systems (the Blackwell "NVL72" racks that bundle GPUs, CPUs, and networking into one unit), and, more sharply, as a one-time charge tied to China cut a single quarter's GAAP margin to 60.5%.24 Hold that thread; it leads directly to the export-control section.

The moat, named precisely

"Nvidia has a strong moat" is not analysis. The moat has a specific mechanism, and it is worth naming exactly, because the durability of the whole investment case rests on it. It is a two-sided ecosystem lock-in with a high switching cost, and its name is CUDA.

CUDA Nvidia's proprietary software platform, launched in 2006, that lets developers run general-purpose parallel code on Nvidia GPUs — and only on Nvidia GPUs. It is the layer that turns the silicon into something programmable, and it does not run on anyone else's chips.

The lock works in three reinforcing layers. First, the libraries. On top of CUDA sit the tools practitioners actually call — cuDNN for neural networks, cuBLAS for linear algebra, TensorRT for inference — part of what Nvidia says are more than 900 "CUDA-X" libraries.15 The dominant machine-learning frameworks are tuned first and best against these. Porting a large training or inference stack to a competitor's software means rewriting and re-validating against a less mature alternative, and the engineering teams capable of doing that are scarce and expensive. That is the switching cost, and it is measured in quarters of lost work, not dollars off a price list.

Second, the flywheel. Nvidia's Jensen Huang has said some six million developers across more than 200 countries have used CUDA since 2006, atop an installed base of hundreds of millions of compatible GPUs.15 More developers write more software for the platform, which makes the platform more valuable, which draws more developers — a compounding loop that a challenger cannot short-circuit by simply building a faster chip, because the chip is not what customers are locked to.

Third, the system. Nvidia has extended the moat from the chip to the whole rack. Its $6.9 billion acquisition of Mellanox, announced in 2019 and closed in April 2020, brought InfiniBand and Ethernet networking in-house;10 on top of that sit NVLink, the high-speed interconnect that lets dozens of GPUs act as one, and a growing software-and-networking stack sold as a complete "AI factory" rather than a component. The pitch to a buyer is no longer "buy our GPU" but "buy our rack, our network, and our software, all co-designed." The competitive effect is that Nvidia holds an estimated 80–90% of the AI-accelerator market by revenue, with training share above 90%.18 That is not a lead; it is, for now, a near-monopoly on the picks and shovels of the entire industry.

The one risk the moat cannot patch: export controls

A software moat defends against competitors. It does nothing against a government that can simply forbid a sale. The single largest discrete risk to Nvidia is not AMD or a custom chip; it is the US Department of Commerce's Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS), which regulates what advanced computing hardware Nvidia may export, and to whom, under the Export Administration Regulations. The rules bind Nvidia as a US exporter, they have tightened roughly once a year since 2022, and each turn has cost the company a redesign or a writedown.

Export Administration Regulations (EAR) The US rulebook, enforced by the Commerce Department's BIS, that controls exports of sensitive technology. For advanced AI chips it sets performance and interconnect thresholds; a chip above them cannot be shipped to specified countries — chiefly China — without a license.

The timeline is a game of the government raising the bar and Nvidia limbo-ing under it. On 7 October 2022, BIS issued its first advanced-computing controls, which banned the sale of Nvidia's top A100 and H100 accelerators to China; Nvidia responded with cut-down A800 and H800 parts engineered to fall just under the thresholds.1112 On 17 October 2023, BIS tightened the rules — a new "performance density" metric, the interconnect loophole closed — banning the A800 and H800 in turn.1314 Nvidia then built the further-degraded H20 specifically for the Chinese market.

In 2025 the vise closed on that chip too, and this is where the policy risk stops being abstract and starts showing up in the accounts. On 9 April 2025 the government told Nvidia that exporting the H20 to China would now require a license, and on 14 April it said the requirement would stand "for the indefinite future."5 Nvidia disclosed it would take a charge of up to roughly $5.5 billion.5 When the quarter closed, the actual charge was $4.5 billion, and the company said roughly $8.0 billion of H20 revenue had been stripped from the following quarter's guidance — the write-down that pushed GAAP gross margin down to 60.5% for the period.4

Then the politics turned again. Around mid-July 2025 the administration signalled it would grant H20 export licenses after all,21 and in August it emerged on unusual terms: Nvidia (and AMD) would remit 15% of their China H20 revenue to the US government as a condition of the licenses — a novel "pay-to-export" arrangement with no clear precedent.20 For an owner, the lesson is not which way the last ruling went but the structural fact underneath: a market that was about 13% of revenue in fiscal 2025 is now a binary, politically-controlled variable that Nvidia's guidance has at times assumed at zero.3 The moat holds everywhere except here.

The bull and the bear, at their strongest

A reader should not be able to tell which way this dossier privately leans, so here are both cases in the words of their most credible holders. The bull case rests on the sheer scale and duration of the buildout; the bear case rests on three specific cracks — custom silicon, margin normalization, and the possibility that the whole cycle is overbuilt.

Morgan Stanley (bull) The market still underestimates how large and how long the AI-infrastructure buildout runs.

The bank has carried a bull-case target around $330 and estimated Nvidia could generate on the order of $1.07 trillion in cumulative data-center revenue across calendar 2025–2027; Wedbush's Dan Ives has argued similarly for a $250 base case.19

Daniel Newman, Futurum Group (bear on share) Custom chips built by Nvidia's own biggest customers will grow faster than the merchant GPU market and erode its share.

Newman has argued cloud ASIC shipments are growing faster than GPUs; the named programs are real and shipping — Google's TPU, Amazon's Trainium, Microsoft's Maia — and each hyperscaler that moves inference to its own silicon is a customer Nvidia partly loses. AMD's MI300-class parts add a second front.1822

Michael Burry, Scion (bear on the cycle) This is a capex bubble with Nvidia as its Cisco, and the hardware's true economics are being flattered.

Disclosing a short position in 2025, Burry called Nvidia "a Cisco at the center" of the buildout and argued that hyperscalers depreciate GPUs over four-to-six years when the real useful life, given Nvidia's annual cadence, is nearer two-and-a-half to three-and-a-half — understating costs and setting up writedowns. Nvidia pushed back publicly on the "bubble" framing.1617

The honest synthesis is that both sides are describing the same fact from opposite ends. Nvidia's margins and share are extraordinary because the moat is real; those same extraordinary numbers are what draw the capital — custom silicon, AMD, and the hyperscalers' own chip teams — that will, in time, compete them down. Which force wins, and when, is the actual investment question. Nothing in the filings settles it.

Go deeper

Do not take this dossier's numbers on faith; the primary documents are public and readable. Start with Nvidia's most recent 10-K and quarterly results on SEC EDGAR and the investor site for the segment detail and the margin bridge.31 For the export-control mechanism, read the BIS press releases and the Congressional Research Service's timeline rather than the coverage of them, so you can see exactly which threshold moved and when.1113 To judge the moat for yourself, watch one metric each quarter that the headlines skip: gross margin. It is the single cleanest signal of whether the pricing power — and therefore the moat — is intact, softening under rack-scale mix, or breaking under competition. When that number moves durably, the thesis moves with it.2
