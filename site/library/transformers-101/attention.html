Attention Is the Engine

One arithmetic trick lets a model read every word of a sentence at the same time — and it is the reason modern AI works.

10 min read2026-07-07

Where we are starting

This course builds a modern language model from the ground up, one idea at a time, for a reader who has never trained a neural network. We start where the field itself restarted: a 2017 paper from Google researchers titled, with unusual confidence, Attention Is All You Need.1 Almost every system you have heard of — the model behind ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — is a direct descendant of the eight-page architecture that paper described. If you understand its one central mechanism, called attention, the rest of the course is detail hung on that spine.

In this lesson
  • Explain, in plain words, why reading text one word at a time was the bottleneck attention removed.
  • Trace what the model actually computes when it "pays attention," using a single running sentence.
  • Read the real scaled-dot-product attention formula and say what each piece does.
  • Name the concrete numbers — parameters, context length — that connect the 2017 model to today's.

One term of art before we begin. A token is the unit a language model reads: roughly a word or a word-piece. "Attention is the engine" is five tokens. Everything that follows is about how a model decides which tokens matter to which.1

The problem: reading one word at a time

Before 2017, the best language models were recurrent networks. A recurrent network reads a sentence the way you might read a ticker tape: one token, then the next, carrying a running summary — a "hidden state" — forward as it goes. To use the tenth word it must first pass through the previous nine, in order.

That design has two costs, and the 2017 paper laid them out precisely in a comparison table.1 First, the work cannot be spread across many processors, because step ten depends on step nine: the paper counts a recurrent layer as needing O(n) sequential operations for a sentence of n tokens, meaning the number of unavoidable one-after-another steps grows with the length of the sentence. Second, for word ten to be influenced by word one, information has to survive nine hand-offs — a long chain along which meaning gets diluted.

The sequential bottleneck A recurrent model must process tokens in order, so long-range links between words are both slow to compute and easy to lose. Attention was designed to break exactly this constraint.

The fix the paper proposed sounds almost too simple: stop reading in order. Let every token look at every other token directly, in one parallel step. In the paper's own accounting, self-attention needs only O(1) sequential operations and connects any two positions with a path of length O(1) — a single hop instead of n.1 The question is what "looking at every other token" actually means as a calculation.

What "attention" actually is

Start with a sentence the original Google team used to explain their own model:6

"The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired."

What does "it" refer to? The animal. Change one word — "because it was too wide" — and "it" now means the street. To resolve the pronoun, the model has to let the word "it" inspect the other words in the sentence and decide which one it depends on. Google's own visualization of the model showed exactly this: when encoding "it," the network puts most of its weight on "animal" in the first sentence and shifts that weight to "street" in the second.6 That act — one token weighing all the others and pulling in information from the ones that matter — is attention.

To do this arithmetically, the model gives every token three vectors, each just a list of numbers it learns during training:

  • A query: what this token is looking for. ("I am a pronoun; who is my referent?")
  • A key: what this token offers to others. ("I am a noun, an animal.")
  • A value: the actual content this token contributes if attended to.
Self-attention Every token produces a query, a key, and a value. Each token's query is compared against every token's key to decide how much to borrow from every token's value. "Self" means the sentence is attending to itself.

The comparison is a dot product — multiply the query and key element by element and add up the result, a single number that is large when two vectors point the same way. "It" 's query lined up against "animal" 's key gives a high score; against "street" 's key, a low one. Those scores, once normalized, are the attention weights, and the model uses them to take a weighted average of the value vectors. High-scoring tokens dominate the average; the rest fade. That average is the new, context-aware representation of "it."1

Inside the mechanism, step by step

The 2017 paper compresses the whole operation into one line, which it calls scaled dot-product attention:1

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √dk) V

It looks dense, but you already understand every piece. Here it is as an ordered mechanism.

  1. Score every pair

    Multiply each query against every key (that is what QKᵀ does in one matrix step). For an n-token sentence you get an n×n grid of scores: how much each token should attend to each other token.1

  2. Divide by √dk

    Here dk is the length of the key vectors. The paper divides the scores by its square root because, for long vectors, raw dot products grow large and push the next step into a range where learning stalls; scaling keeps the numbers well-behaved. In the base model dk = 64, so the scores are divided by 8.1

  3. Softmax into weights

    The softmax function turns each row of scores into positive fractions that sum to one — a set of attention weights. Now "it" might place 0.7 of its attention on "animal" and spread the remaining 0.3 across the sentence.1

  4. Mix the values

    Multiply those weights by the value vectors and add them up. Each token walks away holding a blend of the tokens it cared about — context folded directly into its representation.1

One refinement makes it work in practice. A single attention pass can only track one kind of relationship at a time, but language has many at once — grammar, reference, tense. So the model runs several attention operations in parallel, each with its own learned queries, keys, and values, and stitches the results together. The paper calls this multi-head attention and uses h = 8 heads in its base model, each working in a 64-dimensional slice of the model's 512-dimensional representation.1 One head can specialize in "which noun does this pronoun point to" while another tracks "which verb governs this subject."

Multi-head attention Several attention operations run side by side, each learning a different type of relationship, then their outputs are combined. Eight heads in the original; up to 96 in GPT-3.3

That is the entire engine. The original Transformer stacked this block six times over in its encoder and six in its decoder, wrapped each with a small feed-forward network and some bookkeeping, and trained the result. At roughly 65 million adjustable numbers (parameters) for the base model and 213 million for the larger one, it reached a new state of the art on English-to-German and English-to-French translation — 28.4 and 41.8 on the standard BLEU quality scale — after just 3.5 days of training on eight GPUs.1 The point was not only that it was good, but that it was good cheaply, because attention parallelizes where recurrence cannot.

Why this one idea scaled the world

Because attention removes the sequential chain, you can pour far more computation at a model and it will actually use it. That is the thread connecting the 2017 model to everything since. The architecture barely changed; it just got bigger.

65MPARAMS · TRANSFORMER 2017
1.5BPARAMS · GPT-2 2019
175BPARAMS · GPT-3 2020

GPT-2, in 2019, was the same transformer block scaled to 1.5 billion parameters across 48 layers, reading up to 1,024 tokens at a time.45 A year later GPT-3 pushed to 175 billion parameters, 96 layers, and 96 attention heads, and consumed roughly 3.14×10²³ arithmetic operations — about 3,640 "petaflop/s-days" — to train.3 Nothing conceptual separates it from the 2017 model in this lesson; it is the same four steps, run wider and deeper.

There is one price for attention's power, and it sets up the rest of the course. Scoring every token against every other token means the work grows with the square of the sentence length — the paper lists self-attention's cost as O(n²·d) per layer.1 Double the context and you quadruple the attention cost. That quadratic wall is exactly why the length of text a model can hold in view — its context window — was a hard-won engineering prize, climbing from 2,048 tokens in GPT-3 to 128,000 in GPT-4 Turbo, 200,000 in Claude 3, and a million or more in Gemini 1.5.378910
