Landmark Papers

Attention Is All You Need (2017)

The architecture bet aged extraordinarily well; several of the paper's specific engineering choices did not.

8 min read2026-07-07

What this paper was, and why it still matters

In June 2017, eight researchers at Google published a paper with a title that reads, in hindsight, like a manifesto: Attention Is All You Need.1 It proposed an architecture — the Transformer — that has since become the substrate of almost every frontier AI system in language, vision, and biology. This appraisal asks the only question worth asking about a nine-year-old landmark: not whether it mattered, but which of its specific claims have survived contact with everything published since.

In plain language

At the time, the best translation systems read a sentence word by word, in order, using networks that passed a running memory forward. This paper threw out that sequential machinery and replaced it with a single mechanism — attention — that lets every word look directly at every other word at once. The result beat the state of the art on two standard translation benchmarks while training in a fraction of the time, because the new design could be spread across many processors at once.1

A note on scope, because the paper's reputation now vastly exceeds what it actually claimed. This was a paper about machine translation. It said nothing about pretraining, general-purpose models, scaling laws, or "foundation models" — all of that came later. Read fairly, it is the seed, not the harvest.1

What it actually demonstrated

Strip away the reputation and the paper is a tight empirical result on two 2014 translation tasks. Its "big" model reached a BLEU score — the standard 0–100 measure of translation quality against human references — of 28.4 on English-to-German and 41.8 on English-to-French, both new states of the art; the smaller "base" model scored 27.3 and 38.1.1 The base model held roughly 65 million parameters, the big one 213 million.1

The headline was not only quality but cost. The big model reached that French result after 3.5 days on eight NVIDIA P100 GPUs — a small fraction of the compute rival systems needed.1 The peer-reviewed NeurIPS version carried the same numbers.2

Self-attention A layer in which every position in a sequence computes a weighted combination of all other positions, the weights set by learned similarity. No recurrence, no convolution — every token connects to every other in one step.

The mechanism the paper leans on for its argument is a comparison table that is easy to miss and central to everything. It scores layer types on three axes. A recurrent layer needs O(n) sequential operations for a sentence of n tokens — steps that must happen one after another — and up to n hops for one word to influence another. Self-attention needs O(1) sequential operations and connects any two positions in a single hop.1 That is the whole case: the same expressive power, but parallelizable, so you can train it faster and scale it further.

Crucially, the paper was honest about the catch. Self-attention costs O(n²·d) per layer — the work grows with the square of sequence length — and it noted this is cheaper than recurrence only when the sentence is shorter than the model is wide, "which is most often the case" for the sentence-length inputs it studied.1 That quiet caveat is where much of the paper's later trouble — and a whole research field — would grow.

How it has aged

The architectural bet paid off on a scale the authors did not claim. Within a year, BERT showed that pretraining a Transformer encoder on unlabeled text and fine-tuning it set new records across language understanding, pushing the GLUE benchmark to 80.5%.3 By 2020, GPT-3 was a 175-billion-parameter Transformer that learned tasks from a few examples in its prompt,4 and Kaplan and colleagues had shown that Transformer loss falls as a smooth power law across more than seven orders of magnitude of model size, data, and compute — turning "make it bigger" into a principled program.5 The architecture then jumped domains: a pure Transformer on image patches matched the best convolutional networks,6 and AlphaFold2's attention-based network predicted protein structures at accuracy competitive with experiment.7 Almost nothing in that list is machine translation, and all of it runs on the block this paper defined.

But a fair appraisal separates the durable core from the specific choices that were quietly replaced. Three of the paper's particulars did not survive.

First, the O(n²) cost it flagged became the field's central bottleneck as context windows grew, and it spawned a large "efficient attention" literature: Longformer made attention scale linearly with a sliding window,8 Performer approximated it in linear time,9 and FlashAttention made exact attention memory-linear by rethinking how it moves data on the GPU.10 That last point is the sharp one: much of the Transformer's later practicality came from systems engineering the 2017 paper never anticipated.

Second, its positional scheme was superseded. The paper's own ablation found learned and fixed sinusoidal position encodings "nearly identical,"1 and modern models discarded both in favor of rotary embeddings (RoPE), now standard in open LLMs,11 or ALiBi, which lets a model trained on short sequences run on longer ones.12 Third, the intuitive promise that attention weights show what a model "looks at" was actively contested: Jain and Wallace argued the weights are not faithful explanations,13 and Wiegreffe and Pinter rebutted that it depends on what you mean by explanation.14 The debate remains unsettled.

What holds up
  • The Transformer block — multi-head self-attention, a position-wise feed-forward network, residual connections — is essentially unchanged at the core of frontier models today.4
  • The parallelization argument was correct and load-bearing: removing recurrence is what let the architecture scale with compute.5
  • The bet generalized far beyond translation — to language understanding, vision, and structural biology.67
What to be careful about
  • The vanilla O(n²) attention it shipped is not what runs at scale today; efficient and IO-aware variants do.10
  • Its sinusoidal positions were replaced by RoPE and ALiBi.1112
  • Its ablations were modest — one language pair, small deltas — and it offered no scaling study; the factors that later mattered most were untested here.5

How much weight to put on it today

The honest reading holds two facts at once. The paper's architectural contribution is among the most important in the field's history and is still live in production. Its account of why Transformers win is thin, because the decisive ingredients — scale, pretraining objectives, modern positional schemes, GPU-aware kernels — are all post-2017. Treat it as the founding blueprint, not the finished explanation.

Verdict Put maximum weight on the Transformer block as a design and near-zero weight on the paper as a complete theory of modern AI. The architecture is the seed of the era; the harvest was grown by a decade of work the paper did not foresee.5

What would change this assessment? A non-attention architecture — a state-space model in the Mamba lineage, or a recurrent-retrieval hybrid — decisively beating Transformers at frontier scale across modalities would retroactively reframe this paper as an important-but-transitional step rather than a permanent foundation.6 Conversely, continued dominance of the unmodified attention core through the next model generation strengthens the "landmark that defined the era" reading. As of 2026, the core still stands.
